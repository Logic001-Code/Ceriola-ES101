Overview

Kuratsa is a lively Filipino folk dance from the Visayas, particularly associated with Samar and Leyte. It is a courtship dance that combines playful movements with quick, energetic steps. The dance is usually performed by a male and female pair, and it is known for its improvisational style and spirited rhythm.

History

Kuratsa developed among the Waray people of Eastern Visayas and was traditionally performed during fiestas, weddings, and social gatherings. The dance reflects both Spanish influence and local Filipino creativity, especially in its rhythm and playful interaction between partners. Over time, Kuratsa became a popular social dance and a symbol of celebration in Visayan communities.

Costume

Female dancers typically wear a kimona and patadyong, traditional Visayan attire that allows free movement. Male dancers wear a camisa de chino paired with trousers. The costumes are often brightly colored, emphasizing the festive and energetic nature of the dance.

Cultural Significance

Kuratsa represents joy, courtship, and community celebration. The playful interaction between dancers reflects Filipino values of romance, humor, and social bonding. Today, Kuratsa remains one of the most well-known Visayan folk dances and is commonly performed during cultural festivals, school programs, and traditional celebrations, preserving the vibrant heritage of the Visayas.
