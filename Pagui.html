Overview

Pagui is a traditional Mindanaoan folk dance performed by the Bagobo people of Davao and surrounding areas. It is a ritual dance that often involves praying for blessings, offering thanks, or celebrating harvests. The dance features slow and ceremonial movements, usually performed by a group of dancers in a circular formation, accompanied by traditional percussion instruments.

History

Pagui originated among the Bagobo communities as part of their ancestral rituals and spiritual practices. Historically, it was performed during important occasions such as harvests, community gatherings, and rites of thanksgiving. The dance reflects the spiritual beliefs and customs of the Bagobo people, connecting the community with nature and their deities.

Costume

Dancers wear traditional Bagobo attire, which includes woven skirts, beadwork accessories, and headdresses. The costumes are often colorful and elaborate, highlighting ethnic identity and craftsmanship. Both male and female dancers wear garments that allow for ceremonial gestures and coordinated group movements.

Cultural Significance

Pagui serves as a symbol of spirituality, gratitude, and community cohesion. The dance preserves the ritualistic and cultural heritage of the Bagobo people and emphasizes the importance of tradition, nature, and collective identity. Today, Pagui is performed in cultural festivals, educational programs, and heritage events, helping to keep Mindanaoan folk traditions alive.
